Founded in 2009 by Alex Shevchenko and Max Lytvyn, Grammarly started as a grammar checker for students. By 2026, it has evolved into a comprehensive AI writing platform used across professional, academic, and creative contexts by over 50 million daily active users.

## Key Features: What Grammarly Actually Does

### ✍️ Feature 1: Grammar and Spelling Checker — The Foundation

This is where Grammarly built its reputation — and where it remains best-in-class in 2026.

Grammarly detects 250+ types of grammar, punctuation, and spelling errors — far beyond what Microsoft Word or Google Docs catch. More importantly, it does not just underline errors. It explains why something is wrong and how the correction improves your sentence.

What Grammarly specifically catches:
* Grammar errors — subject-verb agreement, tense consistency, misplaced modifiers
* Punctuation mistakes — comma placement, semicolons, apostrophes, quotation marks
* Spelling errors — including commonly confused words (their/there/they're, affect/effect)
* Passive voice overuse — flagging passive constructions and suggesting active alternatives
* Fragmented sentences — incomplete thoughts that confuse readers
* Run-on sentences — overly long sentences that should be split
* Wordiness — phrases that can be expressed more concisely
* Redundancy — unnecessary repetition of ideas or words

The accuracy is genuinely impressive. In independent testing by multiple publications, Grammarly consistently outperforms competing grammar tools on detection rate and suggestion quality for formal English writing.

One particularly valuable aspect: Grammarly's corrections teach you to write better over time. Unlike tools that simply fix errors silently, Grammarly explains the rule behind each correction — so you gradually make fewer of the same mistakes.

### 🎯 Feature 2: Tone Detection — Grammarly's Unique Superpower

This is the feature that no other writing tool matches — and the one that consistently surprises users the most.

Grammarly analyzes your writing holistically and tells you exactly how it sounds to readers. It detects and labels tone characteristics such as: Confident, Friendly, Formal, Diplomatic, Analytical, Assertive, Optimistic, Urgent, or Direct.

It then provides suggestions to adjust your tone if it does not match your intended communication style.

Real-world examples of where this matters:
* **Email to a client:** Grammarly might flag your draft as sounding "passive-aggressive" or "dismissive" — pointing out specific phrases causing that impression and suggesting professional alternatives you would not have caught yourself.
* **Blog post introduction:** If you want to sound "friendly and approachable" but your writing is reading as "formal and distant," Grammarly identifies the specific sentences creating that disconnect.
* **Performance review:** Grammarly can flag if your feedback sounds "too harsh" or "too vague" — helping you deliver difficult messages in a constructive, professional tone.

### 🤖 Feature 4: GrammarlyGO — Generative AI Writing

Available on the Pro plan, GrammarlyGO is Grammarly's generative AI feature — allowing you to prompt Grammarly to write, rewrite, or improve content directly.

What GrammarlyGO can do:
* Rewrite paragraphs — "Make this more concise," "Make this sound more confident," "Simplify this for a general audience"
* Generate content from prompts — "Write a professional email declining this meeting invitation," "Write an introduction for a blog post about web hosting"
* Improve clarity — identify and restructure confusing sentences or paragraphs
* Create outlines — generate content structures for blog posts, reports, or emails
* Match your personal writing style — GrammarlyGO learns from your writing patterns and generates content that sounds like you

Important realism check: GrammarlyGO is not as powerful as dedicated AI writing platforms like Jasper AI or Claude for long-form content generation. Where it excels is quick, contextual rewrites and improvements within your existing writing — rather than generating complete articles from scratch.

For bloggers, the most practical use of GrammarlyGO is rewriting weak paragraphs, strengthening introductions and conclusions, and generating subject lines or caption variations — quick tasks where having AI assistance directly inside your writing environment is significantly faster than switching to a separate tool.

### 📋 Feature 5: Clarity and Readability Suggestions

Beyond grammar, Grammarly continuously evaluates the readability and clarity of your writing — flagging issues that are technically grammatically correct but still make your writing harder to understand.

What clarity suggestions address:
* Overly complex sentences — long, convoluted structures that should be split or simplified
* Unclear pronoun references — "it" or "they" where the antecedent is ambiguous
* Weak verbs — "is," "are," "was" replaceable with more precise action verbs
* Filler phrases — "in order to," "due to the fact that," "at this point in time" — all simplifiable
* Jargon overuse — technical terms used where simpler language would serve better
* Hedge word overuse — "perhaps," "somewhat," "kind of" — phrases that undermine confidence

For bloggers and content creators specifically, readability improvements directly affect your Google rankings. Google's quality raters evaluate content for clarity and helpfulness — content with clear, accessible writing consistently outranks technically accurate but confusing alternatives.

### 🔍 Feature 6: Plagiarism Checker (Pro Only)

Grammarly Pro includes a plagiarism detection tool that checks your content against billions of web pages and academic sources.

For bloggers using AI writing tools like Jasper or ChatGPT to assist with content creation, this is a critical quality check. AI tools occasionally produce phrases that closely match published sources — Grammarly's plagiarism checker catches these before your content goes live.

For students submitting academic work, the plagiarism checker is potentially worth the entire Pro subscription cost by itself.

The checker identifies:
* Exact matches — verbatim text from published sources
* Near-matches — slightly modified text that is still too similar to source material
* Source citations — where content appears to come from, with URLs for verification
